CSCI339001 Visualization

Data can capture a snapshot of the world and allow us to understand ourselves and our communities better. With ever-increasing amounts of data deeply immersed in our lives, the ability to understand and communicate data is now becoming essential for everyone. Visualization leverages our visual perception to provide a powerful yet accessible way to make sense of large and complex data. It has been widely adopted across different disciplines, from science and engineering to business and journalism, to combat the overabundance of information in our society.

In this course, students will learn foundational knowledge about how to design effective visualizations for analysis and presentation based on theories and principles from graphic design, perceptual psychology, and cognitive science. Students will also learn practical skills about how to rapidly explore and communicate data using Tableau and build interactive visualization products (e.g., articles, tools, and systems) using web-based frameworks including D3.js and Vega-Lite.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, students will be able to D.A.B:

  1. Design effective visualizations that are readable, engaging, and memorable by considering various factors including data types, questions, audiences, and messages
  2. Articulate design choices for their visualizations and benefits & pitfalls of existing visualizations they encounter in the wild
  3. Build interactive visualizations that can address real-world needs, ranging from supporting exploratory analysis to communicating messages about data

Schedule

Week 1

Tue, 8/27📖 The Value of Visualization

This class will provide a general introduction to visualization. What is visualization? Why is it important? What are different functions of visualization? We will answer these questions using historical examples.

Thu, 8/29⚒ Web Development Basics

What skills do we need for visualization development for the web? This lab will introduce HTML & CSS, the most basic building blocks for creating a web page.

Week 2

Tue, 9/3📖 Design Principles

What are general rules of thumb for good visualization design? This class will introduce several design principles for effective visualization design through diverse examples of good, bad and weird visualizations.

Thu, 9/5⚒ Introduction to Javascript

How to make a web page dynamic and interactive? In this lab, students will learn basics of the Javascript language (e.g., loops and functions) and how to create an interactive bar chart.

Week 3

Tue, 9/10📖 Visual Encoding Design

What are building blocks of a visualization? This lecture introduces visual marks and perceptual channels and provides a systematic way to encode an effective visualization and decode existing visualizations in the wild.

Thu, 9/12⚒ Introduction to SVG & D3

Building a visualization requires technology beyond simple HTML, CSS, and Javascript. This lab will give an introduction to how to generate vector-based graphics and bind data to the graphics using D3.js.

Week 4

Tue, 9/17📖 Exploratory Data Analysis

What do we first do when we have data? We explore the data to gain insights about the data. This lecture provides the role of visualizations for exploratory analysis beyond formal statistical modelling and testing.

Thu, 9/19⚒ D3 Scales and Axes

How do we map data to pixels? Students will learn about scale functions to handle the mapping of different types of data, as well as axes to generate visual guides for reading the scales.

Week 5

Tue, 9/24📖 Storytelling with Data

Visualization is not just for analyzing data but for communication of it. What are strategies for telling compelling stories with data? This lecture will take a look at different types of data stories and basic design guidelines.

Thu, 9/26⚒ D3 Update Pattern

What do we do when new data comes in? This lab will tackle the general update pattern for populating new data to an existing visualization, which is at the heart of the D3 library design.

Week 6

Tue, 10/1📖 Task Analysis and Evaluation

Effective visualization depends on specific tasks to be performed on. In this lecture, we will look at a formal methodology to translate high-level goals into low-level tasks, as well as different evaluation methods.

Thu, 10/3⚒ Layouts in D3

Advanced visualizations beyond simple statistical charts often require complex layouts. In this lab, students will learn to use space more effectively by experimenting with commonly used layouts.

Week 7

Tue, 10/8📖 Interaction

How can we explore complex and multi-faceted data in a limited screen space? In this lecture, we will discuss different interaction techniques that allow us to dynamically filter data and coordinate multiple views.

Thu, 10/10⚒ Brushing and Linking in D3

The fundamental guiding principle for interactive visualization is "Overview first, zoom and filter, then details-on-demand". In this lab, we will implement the principle using a focus + context technique.

Week 8

Tue, 10/15📖 No Class - Fall Break

Thu, 10/17⚒ Custom Visualizations in D3

Standard visualizations may not be able to handle the complexity of your data. This lab provides an opportunity to learn how to go out of the box and invent novel visual encodings.

Week 9

Tue, 10/22📖 No Class - IEEE VIS Conference Travel

TBD: there might be a guest lecture or visit to exhibitions in nearby libraries.

Thu, 10/24⚒ Composition & Multiple Views in D3

To support complex tasks or to see multiple perspectives on data, we need more than one visualization. In this lab, students will learn how to coordinate multiple visualizations.

Week 10

Tue, 10/29📖 Perception & Cognition

Well-designed visualizations leverage human visual perception to amplify our limited cognitive abilities. We will cover a set of perception theories on colors, size estimation, preattentive processing, gestalt principles, and more.

Thu, 10/31⚒ Introduction to Vega-Lite & Observable

While D3 is the Swiss army knife for custom visualizations, it is often overkill for most visualizations. This lab introduces Vega-lite that provides a higher-level grammar for rapidly generating standard visualizations.

Week 11

Tue, 11/5📖 Networks and Maps

Statistical graphics can cover tabular data but not network and geographic data. In this lecture, students will learn about specialized visualizations for graph structures and geographic maps.

Thu, 11/7⚒ Transformation, Encoding, and Cartogram in Vega-Lite

Vega-lite significantly ease the making of maps. In this lab, students will learn how to create different kinds of cartograms (e.g., choropleth and symbol maps) in Vega-lite.

Week 12

Tue, 11/12📖 Multi-Dimensional Data

Data is getting more complex and multi-structured (e.g., multivariate dynamic networks), or even unstructured (e.g., text). In this lecture, we will take a look at more complex visualizations for tables, networks, maps, and combinations of all.

Thu, 11/14 ⚒ Multi-View Composition & Interaction in Vega-lite

While not as flexible as D3, Vega-lite provides several ways to handle dense, high-dimensional data. This lab will cover how to construct multi-view interactive displays through layering, faceting, and concatenation in Vega-lite.

Week 13

Week 14

Statistical models are everywhere nowadays. These models are not deterministic and generate estimates with uncertainty. This lecture will introduce visualizations to provide more credible interpretations of the models.

Prerequisites

There are no official prerequisites for this course, but students are expected to have programming experience. Having experience in web development (i.e., Javascript, HTML, and CSS) is a plus, but not required.

Textbooks

There is no required textbook. Readings will be provided and available online through Boston College Libraries

Logistics


Lectures

The class holds a lecture per week on Tuesday. Lectures introduce fundamental knowledge about designing effective visualizations and articulating design rationales. Some lectures feature short in-class activities.

Labs

The class holds a lab on Thursday. Labs offer hands-on training for building interactive visualizations for the web. Labs are self-guided with written instruction. Students submit individually but help each other.

Readings

Each lecture and lab require pre-class readings. Students are required make commentaries before each class.

Homework

There are three homework assignments as below:

  1. Visualization critique & redesign
  2. Exploratory and explanatory analysis with Tableau
  3. Interactive visualization using D3.js

Final Project

The culmination of this course is the final project in which students build creative visualization products of their own interests. Students are required to present their work in the class and write up a final report in a conference abstract format. An exceptional project will be encouraged to submit to the Late-Breaking Works track in ACM CHI. Please find more information about the final project here.

Course Policy

No laptop policy
Students may not use laptops, tablets, phones or other electronic devices during the lectures except when explicitly asked to do so. There is plenty of evidence that multitasking incurs a high cognitive cost in memory and attention (ref), students who take notes on laptops may learn less than those who take notes on paper (ref), laptops adversely impact not only the people using them, but also those around them (ref).

Mandatory attendance
A lot of learning in this course happens through various activities in both lectures and labs. Not only attending but also being on time is crucial.

Late submission
Lab, homework, and project submissions after respective deadlines are not accepted. Under special circumstances such as medical conditions, family issues, or job interviews, we will allow lab submissions without penalty. Please contact the instructor or TA for such situations. We may require proof of evidence.

Regrade policy
Fair and transparent grading is important. Please contact us know if you think there is an error or unreasonable deduction in your assignment. Only regrading requests within 7 days after the initial grade are accepted.
